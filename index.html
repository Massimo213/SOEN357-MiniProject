Project Overview

The Problem

Over 60% of adults with chronic conditions struggle with medication adherence, missed appointments, and fragmented communication with healthcare providers. Existing apps address single needs â€” reminders OR scheduling OR messaging â€” but no unified experience exists that ties these together into a coherent health companion.

The Goal

Design a mobile app that serves as a single hub for medication management, appointment scheduling, and provider communication â€” specifically for individuals managing chronic conditions like diabetes, hypertension, and heart disease.

My Role

Sole UX/UI Designer â€” responsible for user research, persona creation, journey mapping, wireframing, prototyping, and usability testing planning.

User Research

Understanding the real needs behind chronic condition management required a multi-method research approach combining primary and secondary sources.

ğŸ™ï¸

Semi-Structured Interviews

6 participants (ages 28â€“67) managing chronic conditions (Type 2 diabetes, hypertension, asthma). Interviews lasted 30â€“45 minutes via Zoom, focusing on daily medication routines, challenges with scheduling, and experiences with existing health apps.

Key Finding: 5 out of 6 participants reported using 3+ separate apps/tools to manage their health, leading to "app fatigue" and missed doses.

Online Survey

47 respondents recruited via health-related Reddit communities and Facebook groups. The survey covered medication adherence habits, appointment management methods, and desired app features.

Key Finding: 72% said they would use one unified app if it combined reminders, scheduling, and messaging. 68% cited "forgetting to take medication" as their #1 challenge.

Secondary Research

Review of published studies on medication adherence (WHO, 2023), competitive analysis of Medisafe, MyTherapy, and CareZone, and accessibility guidelines (WCAG 2.1 AA, Apple HIG).

Key Finding: WHO reports that medication adherence in developed countries averages only 50%. The most successful apps use contextual reminders (not just alarms) and social accountability features.

Research Synthesis â€” Key Themes

1

Fragmentation

Users juggle multiple tools â€” paper, phone alarms, separate apps â€” with no single source of truth.

2

Cognitive Load

Complex medication schedules (3â€“7 daily meds) overwhelm users, especially elderly patients.

3

Communication Gaps

Patients want to reach providers quickly for non-emergency questions but feel blocked by phone trees and portals.

4

Emotional Burden

Managing chronic conditions daily creates anxiety and guilt â€” apps should reduce stress, not add to it.

Usability Testing

A structured usability testing plan was developed to validate the design and identify friction points before development.

Testing Goals

  1. Validate that users can find and confirm their medications within 10 seconds
  2. Confirm that the appointment flow is intuitive for both tech-savvy and elderly users
  3. Test whether the messaging feature is discoverable without instruction
  4. Measure overall task completion rate and time-on-task for core flows

Participants

5 participants (following Nielsen's recommendation for identifying ~85% of usability issues):

  • 2 participants aged 55+ managing chronic conditions
  • 2 participants aged 25â€“40 managing chronic conditions
  • 1 caregiver who manages medications for a family member

User Tasks

T1
Morning Medication Check-In

"You just took your morning medications. Find them in the app and mark them as taken."

T2
Review Upcoming Appointment

"You have a doctor's appointment today. Find the details and get directions to the clinic."

T3
Message Your Doctor

"You want to ask your doctor about a side effect. Find the messaging feature and send a message."

T4
Check Adherence Progress

"You want to see how well you've been taking your medications this week. Find your progress."

Data Collection Methods

  • Think-Aloud Protocol: Participants verbalize their thoughts while performing tasks
  • Screen Recording: Record all interactions via Maze for replay analysis
  • Task Completion Rate: Binary success/fail per task
  • Time-on-Task: Measure seconds to complete each task
  • SUS (System Usability Scale): Post-test questionnaire (10-item, Likert scale)
  • Post-Task Questions: "What was confusing?", "What would you change?"

Analysis Approach

Feedback would be analyzed using affinity mapping â€” grouping observations into themes (navigation issues, labeling confusion, feature requests). Each issue would be rated by severity (critical, major, minor, cosmetic) using the Nielsen Severity Rating Scale. Critical and major issues would be addressed in the next design iteration before handoff to development.

Reflection

How UX Design Identified User Needs

The UX design process â€” starting from research through to prototyping â€” fundamentally shifted my understanding of what chronic condition patients actually need versus what I assumed they needed. My initial assumption was that the app should be feature-rich with detailed health analytics. But user interviews revealed the opposite: simplicity and reliability were valued far more than feature density.

Sarah, our primary persona, didn't want charts and graphs â€” she wanted to know "what do I take next?" and "when is my appointment?" The journey mapping exercise was especially valuable because it exposed the emotional dimension of health management. Anxiety before appointments, guilt about missed doses, and the relief of having everything in one place â€” these emotional states directly informed design decisions like the calming color palette, the positive reinforcement streak counter, and the pre-appointment checklist.

Challenges Encountered

Balancing Two Distinct User Segments

Sarah (62, low-tech) and James (34, power user) have fundamentally different expectations. Designing for accessibility without making the app feel "dumbed down" for younger users required careful decisions about progressive disclosure â€” showing essential information upfront with detailed data one tap away.

Avoiding Feature Creep

Research uncovered many desired features (symptom journaling, pharmacy integration, insurance tracking). The temptation to include everything would have created the same fragmented experience we were trying to solve. I applied the MoSCoW prioritization framework to keep the MVP focused on medications, appointments, and messaging.

Making Healthcare Communication Feel Safe

The messaging feature raised important UX questions around trust. Users needed visual cues that messages were secure and actually delivered â€” leading to the addition of read receipts, estimated response time, and a "secure" badge on the chat header.

Key Takeaways

01

Research before design, always. Every assumption I had was either incomplete or wrong. The 6 interviews uncovered pain points no amount of competitive analysis would have revealed.

02

Emotion is a design material. Health apps deal with fear, guilt, and anxiety. Ignoring the emotional layer creates sterile tools people abandon. Acknowledging it creates companions people trust.

03

Iterate relentlessly. The paper sketches (Iteration 1) were significantly different from the final high-fidelity mockups. Each round of feedback â€” even informal â€” improved clarity and usability.

04

Accessibility is not an afterthought. Designing for a 62-year-old with reading difficulties from the start made the app better for everyone â€” larger touch targets, clearer hierarchy, and simpler flows benefit all users.
